D, or d, is the fourth letter of the Latin alphabet, used in the modern English alphabet, the alphabets of other western European languages and others worldwide. Its name in English is dee (pronounced /ÀàdiÀê/ ‚ìò), plural dees.[1]

The Semitic letter DƒÅleth may have developed from the logogram for a fish or a door.[2] There are many different Egyptian hieroglyphs that might have inspired this. In Semitic, Ancient Greek[3] and Latin,[4] the letter represented /d/; in the Etruscan alphabet[5] the letter was archaic but still retained. The equivalent Greek letter is delta, Œî.[3]

The minuscule (lower-case) form of 'd' consists of a lower-story left bowl and a stem ascender. It most likely developed by gradual variations on the majuscule (capital) form 'D', and is now composed as a stem with a full lobe to the right. In handwriting, it was common to start the arc to the left of the vertical stroke, resulting in a serif at the top of the arc. This serif was extended while the rest of the letter was reduced, resulting in an angled stroke and loop. The angled stroke slowly developed into a vertical stroke.[6]

In most languages that use the Latin alphabet, ‚ü®d‚ü© generally represents the voiced alveolar or voiced dental plosive /d/.

Computing
The Latin letters ‚ü®D‚ü© and ‚ü®d‚ü© have Unicode encodings U+0044 D latin capital letter d and U+0064 d latin small letter d. These are the same code points as those used in ASCII and ISO 8859. There are also precomposed character encodings for ‚ü®D‚ü© and ‚ü®d‚ü© with diacritics, for most of those listed above; the remainder are produced using combining diacritics.

Variant forms of the letter have unique code points for specialist use: the alphanumeric symbols set in mathematics and science, plosive sounds in linguistics and halfwidth and fullwidth forms for legacy CJK font compatibility.
